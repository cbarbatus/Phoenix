Descriptive Invocation of Deity of the Occasion

 

The story of Saturn is older than Rome itself. Saturn reigned during the Golden Age of Latium, when all people were equal – there was no class distinction and there were no slaves.  Everyone prospered and no one – even kings – set themselves above others.  It was a time when the villages of Latium welcomed among them all who wanted to make their homes and share in the communities’ toil and bounty.

 

Saturn was defeated by his own son, and driven from the celestial realm by his son, Jupiter, fled to Latium.  Just as Latium opened its arms wide to refugees of all kinds, Saturn was welcomed in Italy. Ianus received him as a guest.  He learned from Saturn the art of husbandry, thereby improving his people’s lives, whose methods of farming before then had been brutish and rude.  Saturn is credited with the invention of the art of grafting, with the cultivation of fruit trees, and with instructing men in everything that belongs to the fertilizing of the fields.

 

Ianus and Saturn reigned together in harmony for many a year. It was during their reign that Saturn suddenly disappeared.  Ianus built an altar, instituting rites as to a god and calling these rites the Saturnalia, long before the birth of Rome itself. 

 

A statue of Saturn was filled with oil and was bound with woolen bonds, which were untied for the feast day of Saturnalia.  Those who have associated Saturn with sowing have attributed this unbinding as symbolic of the seed bursting forth in the tenth month (both with respect to the calendar -- December being the tenth month -- and to pregnancy).

 

Let the youngest here among us unbind of Saturn’s feet and so hearken back to the Golden Age of Rome!
