KEY OFFERINGS

 

The story

 

Mike: Jarilo [yaw-ree-low] was a major male Proto-Slavic deity of vegetation, fertility and spring, also associated with war and harvest.  He was a life-death-rebirth deity, (re)born and killed every year. His mythical life cycle followed the yearly life of various wheat plants, from seeding through vegetation to harvest.   Jarilo was a son of the supreme Slavic god of thunder, Perun, his lost, missing, tenth son, born on the last night of February, the festival of Velja Noć [Vell-yah knowch] (Great Night), the pagan Slavic celebration of the New Year. On the same night, however, Jarilo was stolen from his father and taken to the world of dead, where he was adopted and raised by Veles, Perun's enemy, Slavic god of the underworld and cattle.

 

The Slavs believed the underworld to be an ever-green world of eternal spring and wet, grassy plains, where Jarilo grew up guarding the cattle of his stepfather. The land of dead lay across the sea, where migrating birds would fly every winter.  With the advent of spring, Jarilo returned from the otherworld, that is, from across the sea, into the living world, bringing spring and fertility to the land

 

 

Anya: The first of gods to notice Jarilo's return to the living world was Mara [mah-rah], goddess of death and nature, and also a daughter of Perun and Jarilo's twin-sister. The two of them fall in love and court each other through a series of traditional, established rituals. The divine wedding between the brother and the sister, two children of the supreme god, is celebrated in a festival of summer solstice, today known by various in the various Slavic countries - Ivanjski krijesovi [ee-vhan-yis-kee  kree-yes-o-vee] in Croatia, which we celebrate today. This sacred union of Jarilo and Mara, deities of vegetation and of nature, assures abundance, fertility and blessing to the earth, and also brings a temporary peace between the major Slavic gods, Perun and Veles, signifying heaven and underworld. Thus, all prerequisites are met for a bountiful and blessed harvest in late summer which our Grove will celebrate in as the Welsh Alban Elfed .

The Dance - Kaila

 

    The hora is a traditional dance that is popular in Israel and Eastern Europe and enjoyed at weddings and other festive celebrations. The dance is done in a circle with joined hands and uses the 4-count “grapevine” step.

 

    Count 1.  Moving to the right, lift the left foot and cross it in front of the right foot, shifting weight to the left foot.

    Count 2.  Move the right foot right to join the left foot.

    Count 3.  Move the left foot behind the right as weight is shifted to the left foot.

    Count 4.  The right foot joins the left again.

 

and once it has been done for a little while, someone breaks away their right hand and leads the circle across the "bonfires" and then back into the circle.
