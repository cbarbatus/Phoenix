KEY OFFERINGS

The story

LEAD1: Let me introduce you to Jarilo [YAW-ree-low], the focus of our ritual tonight.  He is a very old god, from Proto Slavic times.  He is god of spring, of vegetation and fertility.  But he also is a god of harvest and of war.  He is a life-death-rebirth deity, who is reborn and dies every year. His life cycle follows the yearly life of plants, from seeding through growth to harvest.   Jarilo is a son of the supreme Slavic god of thunder, Perun - his lost, missing, tenth son, born on the last night of February, the festival of Velja Noć [Vell-yah knowch] (Great Night), the pagan Slavic celebration of the New Year. On that same night Jarilo was stolen from his father and taken to the world of dead, where he was adopted and raised by Veles, Perun's enemy, Slavic god of the underworld and cattle.

The Slav’s underworld is an ever-green world of eternal spring and wet, grassy plains, where Jarilo grew up guarding his stepfather’s cattle. The land of dead is across the sea, where migrating birds fly every winter.  With the advent of spring, Jarilo returns from the otherworld, that is, from across the sea, into the living world, bringing spring and fertility to the land.  And so it is that we celebrate Jarilo’s bringing of spring to us.
 

LEAD2: The first of gods to notice Jarilo's return to the living world was Marzanna [Mar-ZHAH-nah], goddess of death and nature. She also is a daughter of Perun and is Jarilo's twin-sister. The two of them fall in love and court each other through a series of traditional, established rituals. The divine wedding between the brother and the sister, two children of the supreme god, is celebrated in a festival of summer solstice, today known by many names in the various Slavic countries - in Croatia it is Ivanjski krijesovi [ee-VHAN-yis-kee  kree-YES-o-vee], which we celebrated at the summer of 2013.
