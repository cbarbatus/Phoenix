BRIEFING, include:

The name of the holiday is Imbolc, which is based on the words for ewe’s milk, because (if you live on a farm in Ireland) the sheep’s udders are beginning to fill with milk in preparation for birthing lambs later in the spring. It’s considered the beginning of the spring in Ireland. Another name for the holiday is Lá Fhéile Bríd, [LAW AY-luh BREEJ] the festival day of Bríd [BREEJ], the Gaelic name of Brigid, Celtic goddess of fire, poetry, fertility and healing.

Brigid has long been so beloved in Ireland that when Christianity took hold there, the folk were loath to give up their relationship with her… so the new church conveniently remembered they had a saint also called Brigid,

Irish Imbolc customs include making a Brídeóg, an effigy of Brigid of straw, dressing it in white, and carrying it around town to bestow her blessings. There’s a customary call and response to invite her in. When we say “Go down on your knees” you can kneel if you want to but you don’t have to.

Fire safety: small pieces of paper, small bits of herbs or incense, small amounts of oil or hard liquor. NO other LIQUIDS please, not even wine or ale, so as not to make vast clouds of smoke that asphyxiate us.

In the tree meditation, when we take each other’s hands, if you don’t want to, cross your arms across your chest.

When we walk in, keep walking around in a circle a few times till everyone has entered. Don’t block the altar or the harp(s) or drums.

Etiquette of giving gifts before we ask for any favors.  When we invite all to give praise or sacrifice, just give praise and thanks and gifts. Later when we sanctify the blessing cup, that’s the time to ask the Kindreds for favors.

Don’t take too long with the cup. Visualize your requests being granted as the blessings flow into the cup, so that when it comes to you, you can just take your sip & pass it along. (Or hold to head or heart or pour a tiny bit on the ground.)

Cup contains alcohol.

THE TALE OF BRIGID’S MANTLE

 

Mike: ‘Mantle’ is another word for cape, or cloak...

In one of the well-known tales about Brigid, she felt called to establish a retreat, a center of welcome, hospitality and peace where all people, especially the poor, could come and find nourishment for life’s journey.  She went to the king of Leinster [LEN-ster] to ask him for some land on which to establish her center.  She told him that the place where they stood was the perfect spot: it was beside an oak grove where she and her companions could collect firewood and berries, there was a lake nearby that would provide water, and the land was fertile, good for raising sheep and cows.

The king, not renowned for generosity, was hesitant and asked her how much land she’d need. “Oh, just as much as I can cover with my mantle,” replied Brigid. This meager request pleased the king, so he readily agreed. Thereupon Brigid took off her mantle and laid it on the ground.  And miraculously, the mantle began to GROW! It grew, and it grew, and much to the king’s astonishment, it continued to grow, until it covered several acres of rich and beautiful land.  The king was dismayed, but honor prevented him from going back on his word. And so was established the town of Cill Dara /Kill DAH-ra/, Irish for ‘the church of the oak’, present-day Kildare, /Kill DARE/ where to this day the Brigidine [BRIDGE-ih-DEEN] sisters keep alight the perpetual flame dedicated to Brigid.
