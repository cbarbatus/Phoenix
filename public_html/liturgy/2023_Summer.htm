" Prihvatite naš prinos darova " as " accept gifts we offer ". (prihvatite - accept, naš-our, prinos-closes to offering ( like yield, part ), darova - gifts.
In this context, whzen used with "darova", "prinos" has less of a "yield" meaning and more as a gift you are bringing .

Pronunciation for "Prihvatite" is the same as you already pronounced, while "naš" just misses "u" at the end so it ends in "sh" - like "nahsh".
"prinos darova " is very fluently pronounced in google translate, it sounds like "pree-nose"  - pri (as in eng."PRIsm" and "nos" as in eng."NOSE").
"darova" pronounces as "dar" - (as in eng. "DARling" )and "ova" (as ending in eng. "supernOVA").

KEY OFFERINGS
 
The story
 
Elizabeth: Jarilo [yaw-ree-low] was a major male Proto-Slavic deity of vegetation, fertility and spring, also associated with war and harvest.  He was a life-death-rebirth deity, (re)born and killed every year. His mythical life cycle followed the yearly life of various wheat plants, from seeding through vegetation to harvest.   Jarilo was a son of the supreme Slavic god of thunder, Perun, his lost, missing, tenth son, born on the last night of February, the festival of Velja Noć [Vell-yah knowch] (Great Night), the pagan Slavic celebration of the New Year. On the same night, however, Jarilo was stolen from his father and taken to the world of dead, where he was adopted and raised by Veles, Perun's enemy, Slavic god of the underworld and cattle.
 
The Slavs believed the underworld to be an ever-green world of eternal spring and wet, grassy plains, where Jarilo grew up guarding the cattle of his stepfather. The land of dead lay across the sea, where migrating birds would fly every winter.  With the advent of spring, Jarilo returned from the otherworld, that is, from across the sea, into the living world, bringing spring and fertility to the land.
 
 
Sarah: The first of gods to notice Jarilo's return to the living world was Mara [mah-rah], goddess of death and nature, and also a daughter of Perun and Jarilo's twin-sister. The two of them fall in love and court each other through a series of traditional, established rituals. The divine wedding between the brother and the sister, two children of the supreme god, is celebrated in a festival of summer solstice, today known by various names in the various Slavic countries – one of which is Kupala, which we celebrate today. This sacred union of Jarilo and Mara, deities of vegetation and of nature, assures abundance, fertility and blessing to the earth, and also brings a temporary peace between the major Slavic gods, Perun and Veles, signifying heaven and underworld. Thus, all prerequisites are met for a bountiful and blessed harvest in late summer which our Grove will celebrate in as the Welsh Alban Elfed .

Elizabeth: Several of the Slavic traditions for midsummer have to do with the picking and using of herbs. One tradition is unmarried girls make crowns of flowers and herbs and set them in the river with a candle at the center and a boy who likes her might take her crown from the river and return it to her, if she takes it back she rejects his offer but if she places the crown on his head she accepts. Another traditions talks of taking an old wagon wheel and weaving herbs and flowers and straw around the spokes lighting it on fire and rolling it down the hill to be doused in the river symbolizing the dousing of the sun as this is the time when we have reached the pinnacle of daylight and the days go back to shortening another tradition states that you should go out at night and pick herbs and these herbs will have magical qualities. A suggestion is to place seven herbs into a pouch and place that pouch with your magical tools and it will keep them protected and charged with magical energy.
