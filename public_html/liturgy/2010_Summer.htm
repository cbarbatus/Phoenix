Opening Statement and Precedence

 

     PD:  We belong to the gods and the gods belong to us, so let us pray with a good fire.  Let us offer with a full heart, knowing that when we do, we nurture our connection to them and support the very gods themselves.  In remembrance, we strengthen their powers.  In honor, we balance the worlds.  In love, we bring peace and keep the Old Ways alive and present. 

 

     Brothers and sisters, welcome to this Nemeton and welcome to this celebration of the Summer Solstice, a time of great shift where the triumph of Light is at its apex, even though the jewels of Darkness are contained within it.  This Solstice marks the beginning of the harvest cycle on the earth and also in our personal and collective harvests.  It is here, right now, where we stand equally in the year, holding the gifts of the seed and the womb, the gifts of beginnings and endings, and the gifts of activity and introspection. 

 

     As we have chosen to gather as a folk here in this sacred threshold-time, let us become a threshold people, for this what magickal people are.  Let us prepare ourselves to preserve and store the blessing of this year’s coming harvest so that we can walk in peace, compassion, health, and strength.  Even though the sun’s strength begins its waning course, let us not fear, but seek to tend it and stoke its warmth within us until its power returns to us again. 

 

     For ultimately, WE ARE THE HARVEST FEAST FOR THE WORLD; our soul and life energy, our deeds and thoughts, are all part of the Great Meal we share.  May this rite give you an opportunity to tend your soul, and to tend your harvest in communion with the Kindred and the community gathered here.

II. Opening of the Grove

Grove Attunement – Two Powers and the Mists

 

Statement of Purpose – Tending/ Healing/preparing

PD:  Working with the world of spirit in sacred time is a feast, a feast where we can experience what we all long for -  a way to sanctify our identity, to walk in community with the Kindred in the complete bliss of being connected and feeling truly alive.  We long for experience that will push us past the boundaries of what we already know into the full life of the soul and the sacred mystery within every existence.

  

     Those of us standing here have already begun to hear the call to cultivate ourselves and to be a bridge for others.  But – in the midst of our searching and offering, how often do we tend to our inner landscapes and gardens?   How can we be spiritual food for others if we do not tend to our own soul-harvests with the same care we to tend to our outer world or the soul-harvest of others?

 

     The Solstice marks the beginning of the harvest time. In Celtic lore, it is the most powerful time for gathering healing herbs as they are considered to be most ripe and potent, filled with the sun, born of his flesh.  This gathering in of the sun’s properties sets a ritual precedent for the larger Harvest that will feed the tribe.  It is here, that we mirror the gods’ actions through honoring the primordial elements of life and the guardian deities of herbalists and physicians.   We will bolster and gather up the healing properties present in our soul-harvests this year so that we may be fed and feed others in the Great Meal of life.

Main Offering

PD:  In keeping with the Old Ways, we strengthen by remembering.  Let us now offer the story of the Tuatha Dé’s great healers that we may enter the tale and walk the world between worlds in their presence.

 

Story of Dian Cécht, Miach, and Airmid

 

     In times of olde, it is told, that Nuadu, the great king of the Tuatha Dé Danann, lost his arm in a battle between his people and the Fomorians.  As was the custom, a leader must be free from any defect, as his perfection would mirror the wellbeing of his people and the bounty of the Land.  His arm had been replaced by a silver one, well wrought by Goibniu, the smith god.  However, though this silver arm functioned aptly, it was still questioned whether that would allow Nuadu the sacred legitimacy to lead his people once again.  In his stead, Bres mac Elathan, a Fomorian, had been allowed to rule.  True to his Fomorian nature, he ruled most cruelly and inhospitably, allowing the Land to become barren. At his hands, the people of Danann suffered terrible insult and hunger.

 

     Dian Cécht, divine physician to the Tuatha Dé had cured Nuadu of his other wounds, but regretfully he had no remedy for Nuadu’s arm.  Dian Cécht’s children, Miach and Airmid, went to offer their services to Nuadu (as they could).  They wished to find a way to restore Nuadu’s arm to its original state.  Miach recovered the hand from where it was buried and placed it on the stump of the silver arm.  He made many an incantation, and in three days, the limb was made whole once again.  The happy news rallied the people of Danann, and so, they secretly began to plan their revolt.

 

     Jealousy took hold of Dian Cécht, and so he sent for his son.  When the dutiful Miach arrived to meet his father, Dian Cécht struck him on his head with a heavy sword three times.  Of these wounds, each was Miach able to heal instantly.  Growing more enraged by his son’s talent, Dian Cécht offered one more blow, much stronger than the others.  This blow destroyed Miach’s brain, and so his life ebbed and was no more.  Miach’s father buried him deep in the earth.  From this grave, many herbs began to grow, outlining a body, with each herb growing from the place where its properties had its strength and function.  Miach and the Nature Spirits had provided a map for all maladies for all the ages. 

 

      Airmid, hearing of her brother’s murder, stole away to his grave, amazed at what she saw there.  She quickly took off her cloak and laid it on the ground, harvesting the herbs and placing them on her mantle in the same location as she found them.  But, her father, still unwilling to acknowledge his son’s healing powers, even in his death, grasped the cloak and scattered the herbs everywhere, confusing the positions and powers of the herbs.  It is said that no disease would be incurable had these properties not been confounded.  From this time on, herbalists and healers would have to be ever-diligent to regain the knowledge that was lost.  It is likely that some of the knowledge will never be recovered.

 

     Now, upon hearing this story, one might wonder why we honor Dian Cécht at all.  Yet, we must acknowledge that Dian Cécht and his son, Miach, reflect the cyclical nature of the sun, in both the sun’s expansive and reflective powers.  It must be remembered that no great gift has ever been brought to mankind without a great sacrifice of some sort and the work of those worthy to dispense the gifts of the Kindred as they are intended.  So it is appropriate that we honor the three – as Druid’s know.   For Nuadu was restored to power and the Tuatha Dé Danann were victorious, living in beauty and abundance once more.  From that time to this, it is said that Dian Cécht and his daughter, Airmid, have worked together to aid those seekers, who walk in wisdom, looking to regain that sacred knowledge.  May their story, being offered and told, strengthen their powers, opening the doors to greater healing in all the worlds.

V. Working: Tending the Harvest, Tending the Soul

PD:  Filled with the blessings of the Kindred, let us spend time in a healing place where we can strengthen the fires of the solstice within us, and tend to our soul and to the souls of others.  Let the fire tender come forth to strengthen our sacred fire with the heat of the solstice sun.  Let us together lend our focus to assist in this act.

2)Meditation

PD:  No matter where we are in our life cycle, we each have a crop to cultivate.  Let us turn inward to expand and strengthen the blessings of the solstice-sun so that we may yield a healthy and bountiful harvest.

PD:  Now that we are strengthened by the therapeutic gifts of this harvest sun, let us hear of the gifts of healing from the earth, gifts that you will take with you this day.

     Following the laying out of the herbs, a brief prayer of thanks is said by the Grove Herbalist.  Then the G.H. will gather the Brat Airmeitha by the corners, stand over the solstice flame, and shake the herbs inside the cloth so that they mix together.  Cloth will be laid out once more, and people will be invited to come forward and harvest some to take home (in Dixie Cups or other).  They must pick up the herbs with their right hand.  When storing at home they must take out the herbs to store with their left.  The G.H. will caution them not to eat or make a tea out of the mixture.  However, putting herbs in bath water for a ritual bath, carrying in a pouch on the person, or scattering the herbs in the yard of their home or a an area of land they would like to help heal, are all actions that are recommended. Additional info will be put in the ritual program.

 

5) Tending each other

PD:  We have basked in the sun and gathered the healing gifts of water, seed, sun, and earth.  As we stand at the brink of our harvest time, let us tend it by connecting with the mystery of each other – offering the most sacred meal. 

 

     Participants will be directed to choose a partner for a simple tactile exercise (holding, rocking) during the song.  They will be asked to meditate in the movement and the moment – being tended to as they are tending another.

 

Bard’s will sing Heaven’s here on Earth.

 

Mantra: I am another you, you are another me. (taken from a Mayan song)

 

PD:  Walking this path takes courage.  It requires us to make more room for the journey of spirit in our lives and to allow time and silence so that hidden gifts may be revealed.  It requires that we be vulnerable and that we experience loss.  Yet, if we keep a place for the blessings of spirit, we will become them, remembering that “we are the harvest and what we tend, will tend us in the end.” (MacEowen)
