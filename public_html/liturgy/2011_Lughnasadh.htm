Introduction:

 

PD1:  Hear us, O Kindred, for The Children of the Earth have gathered in this Sacred Grove to keep the rites of Lughnassadh.  As our ancestors have done before us, we come to this place in fellowship and peace to celebrate the harvest of the Land and to reflect upon our personal harvests and our commitment to the Kindred.   

 

We honor here today the young hero, Lugh, champion of Ireland.  As is custom, we also honor his foster mother, Tailtiu, who gave her life to make easy the way and to feed her people by clearing the plains and ploughing the soil.  At Lughnassadh, the different tribes of Ireland came together at her center to feast and to compete in games of skill while never drawing a sword from its sheath in anger or retribution for past misdeeds or ancient grudges.   

 

Here, we meet today, representing our tribes in the rivers of our blood and the landscapes of our bodies.  Here do we come to reflect upon the interconnectedness of all people, all spirit, all deeds, all time.  Most important of all, we come to honor the Kindred and to recall our oaths to the Land and our people, we come to nurture peace and unity, and we come to meditate upon the fruits of the season - those that ripened and those that have withered on the vine.  Let this rite sustain unity in all the land, and may future generations continue to gather together to remember and to celebrate their harvests in peace and plenty.

Short Statement of Ritual Purpose

 

PD2:  Now we are in the Ripe Time of the year, when the labor of the people brings the Land to bear, and the Power of the Sun gives itself to the Power of the Earth.  We gather here for rest and pleasure as the labor and the lessons become manifest.

 

In this time then, it is fitting that we honor Lugh who is known by many names - for his light shines strong in many lands and his deeds of deliverance from a people’s despair beats happy in many hearts.  He is often remembered at this time for his victory over Balor and the forces that strive against virtue and order.  Today, however, we will remember him with a different story – one that focuses on his role as a fair judge and a great unifier.  Through ritual action, we will seek to strengthen community, emulating his unfailing compassion and resolve in order to forge a stronger bond both to the Land and to humankind.  His long arm, both loved and sometimes scorned, created fertile conditions for the land to bear fruit and for the tribe to live with a common focus in the idea of beauty, which is a part of many spiritual traditions.  This beauty is found in the measured fitness of things and is represented in this druidic rite today by measured word and song.

 

In the Celtic way, this time of harvest contains the shadow-time as well.  Though Lugh is, for us, the bright spear of sunlight who leads us through the dark time of the year and the dark stages of our transformations, he is also the paradox of darkness in light and light in darkness.  Let us take this time together to reflect in sacred space upon what we harvest with our light and with our shadow-selves.

 

So let us pray with a good fire, because we belong to the gods and the gods belong to us.  Let us offer with a full heart, knowing that when we do, we nurture our connection to them and all spirit.  In remembrance, we strengthen and sustain their powers.  In honor, we balance the worlds.  And, in love, we keep peace and the Old Ways alive and present.

The Eric-Fine of Lugh

 

Druid as storyteller recounts story with background music

D1: 

 

The chiefs of the Tuatha De Danaan thronged ‘round Lugh on the Hill of Usna.  Lugh stood high on the summit, and the Sword of Light was bare in his hand.  The rest of the hill below him shone with a radiance like white silver.  “Chiefs,” cried Lugh, “behold, I have the great Sword!  You should have the other three great jewels to match it.  Where are the Spear of Victory, the Cauldron of Plenty, and the Stone of Destiny?”  The great chiefs bowed their heads and veiled their faces from Lugh as they answered, “The Fomor, those thieves of beauty, order, and the generous spirit, have taken the Cauldron and the Spear.  They have degraded all things good and robbed us of our grain.  Ireland, herself, hides the Stone away from them, deep in her flesh.”

 

At the news, Lugh whirled the sword around him until it became a glimmering wheel of light, and he cried to the Earth of Ireland that she might reveal the Lia Faíl, the most precious of sacred stones.  A strong, sweet music welled up from the earth and every stone and leaf and every drop of water shone with light till all Ireland seemed one vast shining crystal, changing from one hue to another while remaining radiant with all color.  Lugh cried, “It is enough, the earth has well answered me, and Ireland has the stone safe in her care.”

 

With this happy news, he turned to the chiefs and encouraged them by saying, “Raise up your foreheads, O Chiefs, though you do not have all the jewels, you have the scars of battle-combat and you have endured sorrow and hardship. You know what it is to be exiled in your own land.  Let us swear brotherhood on the two jewels we have, and let us strive to bring an end to the dishonoring of the Land and all the worlds.  Hold up your hands and swear, as I and those from Tir-na-Og will swear, and the sacred Land will swear, that we may have one mind, one heart, and one desire amongst us all.”  The chiefs lifted up their hands and swore a great oath of brotherhood with the Earth and with the hosts of Shining Ones from Tir-na-Og.  They swore by the stone and the sword of light, by the Fire that is over the earth and under it, and by the fire that is in the heart of heroes.  The hills and valleys, plains and rivers, and the lakes and forests of all Ireland swore it – fastening the bond of brotherhood on themselves willfully.  Thus, did they prepare to hold their bond fast and true.

 

Meanwhile, Cian, Lugh’s father, had gone out on a quest to secure additional help for the Great Battle sure to come.  To his misfortune, he happened upon the three sons of Turann, whose hatred for him was most vile.  Trying to avoid an ugly altercation that would delay him from his important quest, he turned himself into a boar and hid himself with a herd of swine.  The three sons, recognizing him and eager to do mischief, changed themselves into hounds and hunted him.  When Cian was badly wounded, he cried out, “Do not slay me in the form of a pig, Hold!”  Cian took his own form again and spat, “You see my bloody face now, Sons of Turann.  Well you knew me from the first and well I knew you, Oath-breakers!”

 

Urcar, one of the sons of Turann insisted loudly, “The bands of death on your poisonous tongue; take back your words!” 

Cian pronounced, “I will not take them back, Sons of the Adder.  Slay me, and every drop of blood will cry out on you – your very weapons will call out on you in the Place of Assembly.” 

 “Then, we will slay you with weapons that cannot cry out,” said the brothers.  They lifted great stones and rocks from the earth and stoned Cian until he was dead.    

 

The Sons of Turann buried the body of Cian deep in the ground, but the earth refused to hide the body and cast it up again before them.  They sought to bury him deeper each time and six times the earth cast him up.  Upon the seventh time, the earth made no sign, and so, the three murdering kinsman quickly left to be conveniently seen in the company of honorable warriors. 

The earth sent a little breeze to Lugh, touching his face and eyelids, caressing the thick curls of his hair; Ireland’s breath touched his hand as a hound touches the hand of a beloved master, and Lugh knew the wind had come for him.  He followed the beckoning winds until he reached the place where Cian had been slain.  The earth said, “O Lugh, the bond of brotherhood is broken.  The sons of Turann have slain your father.  Look what a poor, torn thing I cover.”  So, the earth laid bare the mangled, blood-stained body of Lugh’s father in the place where the earth was trampled and defiled.  Two tears gathered slowly at Lugh’s eyes, and as he shook them away, the earth sent up a little well of pure water close to him.  He bent over it, saying, “O Earth, forgive the broken bond.”

 

The little spring in the heart of the well leapt in answer, and nine crystalline bubbles rose through the water.  Lugh made a cup of his two hands and lifted water from the well.  He sprinkled it in the torn earth, and greenness came again to the trampled grass.  He sprinkled it on the bruised body of his father, and it became whole and radiant.  “O Earth,” said Lugh, “most noble and beloved, I will avenge your wrong.  Father, you shall yet send help for the battle; now, the hands of your slayers shall bring it.  After all, dear Father, ‘tis not wearisome to wait for news of victory in the Isle of Golden Apples, for all the winds that blow there are winds of beauty, and now you have crimson flowers beneath your feet and the radiance of the Silver Fleece about you.”  Cian’s son tenderly laid the body of his father in the earth and went to seek the slayers at the King’s court.

 

King Nuada sat shining in his royal seat among his warrior band.  His face was joyous as they all were in each other’s company.  But, as Lugh walked into the hall, his heart’s heaviness shook the joy from the assembly.  “Why is the hero-light gone from your forehead, O Lugh, Ildanach,” the king asked.  Lugh explained how he had found his father and how the earth had been desecrated.  Nuada veiled his face and all the chiefs bowed their heads and raised the three sorrowful cries of the keene.  Only the sons of Turann remained upright with haughty eyes and unbowed heads.  At first, the chiefs spoke at their displeasure of the Fomor.  But Lugh explained, “It was not from the Fomor, that my father died – the hands that slew him have sworn the oath of brotherhood.”     

 

At this, the king demanded their names and vowed that though they be the noblest or most loved, they shall perish, as was the way in the days of olde.  Lugh called upon them, “Brian, Ur, and Urcar, the sons of Turann.”  But when the king and Lugh looked upon these brothers, their hearts fell.  Here, the mightiest and most beautiful of the warriors stood before them.  Lugh then insisted that instead of death, they should pay the honor price (eric) instead to make good for the battle-loss of Cian.  The sons agreed to this, and the King was pleased with Lugh’s wisdom, for Lugh would not be able to fight in the Great Battle without this being resolved.  Lugh, however, did not make the price an easy one.   He asked for the seven magical treasures located throughout the world, but he granted the brothers the use of Manannan’s Ocean Sweeper to take them swiftly where they needed to go.

 

With luck, wisdom, and determination, the brothers managed to gather all the items but two.  These two items would have been foregone gladly if the test was passed, for Lugh had put a spell of forgetfulness and remembrance into the seas the brothers traveled.  Had the brothers felt remorse for what they had done to the land, they would have remembered the items remaining, but since they still had no sorrow for their actions they languished into forgetfulness and longed desperately to see the healing shores of their home once more. 

 

When weakness came upon them, and they waited for death Ur spoke, “I wish we were in our dear Ireland, lying on a hillside there, for the Irish hills are gentle and every wind that blows on them is full of peace.”

 

 “We have no part in Ireland,” said Brian, “for we have broken our oath to her.”

 

 “My grief!” cried Urcar, “My bitterest sorrow is that we shall never see the Sacred Land again - Oh, that the earth would forgive us and allow us to rest in her at our end!”

 

As he spoke, a little wind came out of Ireland.  Soft and gentle, it touched the sons of Turann with hope and healing, and it pushed their boat back to the shores of their home.  They were lifted upon the shore, by their family, Lugh himself, and the warriors who attended them in their last moments.  The three kinsmen kissed the sacred earth and said, “O kinsfolk, entreat forgiveness for us from the earth, and friendly burial – even as we now entreat it for ourselves.  Farewell.  Make no lamentation for us.” 

 

When they died, their kinsfolk indeed made great lamentation, but in the Isle of the Fair-folk, Cian greeted the men as brothers and gave to each a golden apple which they ate.  There, they all knew that the Fomor would be defeated in the Great Battle; they all knew the words of the Peace-Chant that Brigid would sing.  “O Cian, dear friend,” said the three kinsmen, “it is surely not hard to wait for news of victory in the Isle of Golden Apples.”

Taken and adapted from Celtic Wonder Tales, “The Eric-Fine of Lugh” by Ella Young (pp.109-118)
