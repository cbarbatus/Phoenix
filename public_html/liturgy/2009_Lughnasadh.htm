Introduction:

 

PD1 [LISA]:  The Children of the Earth are gathered in this Sacred Grove to keep the rites of Lughnassadh.  As our ancestors have done before us, we gather together in fellowship to share in the success of the Harvest.  We gather so that we may celebrate and reflect upon the outcomes of our personal harvests which mirror our relationship between the Tribe and the Land.   

 

     We honor here today, the young hero, Lugh, champion of Ireland, who embodies the perfection of this relationship.  As is custom, we also honor his foster mother, Tailtiu, who gave her life to feed her people by clearing the plains and ploughing the soil.  Lughnasadh was a festival where the different tribes and their chieftains gathered at the center of Ireland to feast and to compete in games of skill, while never drawing a sword from its sheath in anger or retribution for past misdeeds.  These tribes came to also honor the central connection of the Land to Sovereignty.  Here, do we meet today, representing our tribes in the rivers of our blood and the landscapes of our bodies to share and play, to honor the Kindred, to nurture peace and unity, and to partake of the fruits of the season.  We gather here connected to all time in that same spirit of camaraderie, respect, and joy.  Let this rite sustain brotherhood in all the land and accord in our personal and collective growth, and may future generations gather together to remember and to celebrate their harvests in peace and plenty as well.

Short Statement of Ritual Purpose

 

PD2 [MIKE]:      Now is the beginning of the Ripe Time, when the labor of the people brings the Land to bear, when the Power of the Sun gives itself to the Power of the Earth.  We gather here for rest and pleasure before the labor and lessons, of reaping what we have sown, take place.  As we plan to gather in our store for the fallow time to come, may we find that we have sown our seeds well.

 

      In this time of the harvest then, it is fitting that we honor the god of light and grain, Lugh, who is known by many names, for his light shines strong in many lands and many cities are named for him.  In the days of olde in Ireland, a basic truth was well known by all -  that the virtue and generosity of the High King was directly connected to the bounty of the land and the wellbeing of its people.  To the degree that the king was honorable, the land flourished, but if the King was corrupt and selfish, the land, over time, would cease to bear its gifts and the people would suffer. 

 

Lugh became this ideal King, for he brought the skill of all arts to bless and strengthen his people, his virtue made the land blossom, and he became the people’s sacred center, ever mindful of maintaining right relationship with the Goddesses of the Land and of Sovereignty, those who, alone, grant true power and bounty.    

 

Lugh is also for us in this season, the bright spear of sunlight who leads us through this dark time of the year and the dark stages of our transitions and transformations.  He is the paradox of the shadow-time as well the welcoming ray of the light of dawn.

Lugh’s Beginning and the Tale of Tara

 

Druid as storyteller recounts story with background music

 

D1: 

     The story goes that Balor, King of the Formorians of the dark island realms, heard a prophecy from a Druid who revealed, “Balor, you will come to be slain by your own grandson.”  So Balor pondered carefully, “I have only one infant daughter, Ethlinn.  Perhaps, if I find a way to conceal her, my fate will not be sealed in such a manner.”

 

     So, Balor had his daughter removed and imprisoned in the Highest of Towers on the highest of dangerous cliffs overlooking the shore of a tempestuous sea.  He charged twelve strict matrons to watch over her to ensure that she would never see the face of any man or even come to know that there is any other gender other than her own.  Of course, as all sequestered princesses do, she grew into a maid of surpassing beauty and grace.

 

    Now, it happened that there were on the mainland three brothers, Kian, Sawan, and Goban the Smith, who was the great armourer of the people of Danu.  Of these three, Kian had a magical cow whose milk was so abundant that everyone longed to possess her.  Even Balor was determined to get his hands on that cow.  So he disguised himself as a red-haired boy and found a way to distract Sawan who was guarding her.  Balor immediately carried the cow off, dragging her across the sea to Tory Island.  (Liz gives us a startled, Moo!)

 

     Kian, the owner of this marvelous cow, was determined to get her back and to avenge himself on Balor.  He wisely sought the advice of a Druidess named Birōg.  Dressing himself in women’s garb, he was wafted across the sea by a magical spell, accompanied by the druidess.  When they landed on the shore below the tower, they walked to the fortress and, terrified, pleaded with Eithlinn’s dour guardians,  Lady 1: Please have charity; we are two noble ladies who have been running from some gruesome men who are trying to abduct us.  We have been traveling a long time and found our way to this shore to hide.  Lady 2: Please grant us shelter so that we can rest in safety. We beg you and promise to repay your kindness when we are able.”  The matrons were understanding, and so, they warily assented to give them shelter for the night. 

 

     Kian, upon seeing Ethlinn, was determined to find means to access her.  Birog enchanted the matrons and Kian entered her chamber as the women settled themselves into a long, magical slumber. (brief snoring here)  When they awakened, Kian and the druidess had disappeared, but Ethlinn had already given Kian her love and soon her guardians found that she was with child.  Fearing Balor’s wrath, the matrons convinced Ethlinn and themselves that it was only a dream.  However, in due time, Ethlinn birthed triplets, all boys.

 

     News of this reached Balor and he was enraged and frightened and he commanded that the three infants be drowned immediately in a whirlpool off the Irish coast.  The man in charge of the deed wrapped up the children in a sheet, but in carrying them to the appointed place, the pin of the sheet came loose, and one of the children dropped out and fell into a little bay (baby cry – Wahhhh!).  The other two babies were duly drowned and the servant reported that his mission was accomplished.  (Awwwwhhh!)

 

     But the child who had fallen into the bay was under the protection of the Druidess, and so, he magically was wafted home to his father.  Now as was the custom in that day, Kian gave the child in fosterage to his brother the smith who taught the child to be skilled in every manner of craft and handiwork.  This child was Lugh. (Hooray!)  When he was grown to his youth, he showed much promise (ooohh ahhhh!) and the noble Dananns placed him in the charge of Duach, King of the Dark, in the place known as Faeryland or the Land of the Dead, and here he dwelt until manhood.

 

     For many years the Great Ones of the Túatha Dé Danann had been oppressed by the Formorians and the High King Bres, who though of Formorian descent, was given the charge of the kingdom of the Túatha Dé as a gesture of good faith between them (Boo!).  But that is another story for another time.  What is important is that the people of Danu were about to face, not only the battle of their lives against the Formorians, but the most important battle in all of Irish myth.  It was high time that an honorable king sit once again on the High Throne, for the land was becoming barren and the people were suffering terribly (sounds of suffering).  Lugh decided that it was time for him to come to the aid of his father’s people so he made toward the Great Palace of Tara from the Land of the Dead where he had learned much.  (Sound of coconuts or Bonanza theme song)

 

He made his way to the great door of the great palace where a discerning doorkeeper sat guard.  It is said that no one could enter unless there was need of him. 

     Lugh stated, “I have come to take service with the Great Nuada of the Silver Hand.” 

     “What service can you offer, what can you do,” inquired the royal doorkeeper.

     “I am a carpenter,” Lugh replied.

     “We are in no need of a carpenter; we already have an excellent one in Luchta, son of Luchad.”

     “I am a smith, too,” Lugh said confidently.

     “Well, we have a master-smith, already.”  The doorkeeper began to grow impatient.

     “Then, I am a warrior,” Lugh insisted.

     “We do not need one, while we have Ogma,” he taunted.

 

It became clear that the doorkeeper was tiring quickly of this young man’s list.  Still, Lugh went on to name all he could think of:  poet, harper, a man of science, a physician, a spencer, and so forth, only to receive the same answer, that there was already a person of supreme accomplishment in that art already installed at the court of Nuada.  Lugh finally realized that he knew what the court was missing.  So he said firmly, “Then, ask the King, if he has in his service any ONE man who is accomplished in every ONE of these arts, and if he has, I shall stay here no longer, nor seek to enter his palace.”  Upon this, Lugh was received and the surname Ildánach was conferred upon him, meaning the “all craftsman” or “Prince of all Sciences.”

 

What Lugh didn’t mention at the royal door, was that he also brought with him many magical gifts from the land of Faery.  There was the Boat of Mananan Mac Lir, which knew a man’s thoughts and could travel anywhere a man wished.  There was the Horse of Mananan that could travel over land and sea alike.  Last, but certainly not least, was the terrible sword named Fragarach, “The Answerer,” that could cut through any chainmail or armor.  So equipped, he appeared one day before the assembly of Danann chiefs who were meeting with the envoys of their Formorian oppressors in order to pay their required tribute (the tribute requirements were always very high and demanded everything most dear to the people of the Túatha Dé).  Under Lugh’s leadership, the chieftains attacked the Formorian envoys, and they had slain all but nine men.  These men were left to go back to tell Balor of his new foe.  Balor’s rage was beyond ugly and he made ready for the mother of all battles.  (Balor Growls Hideously!)

 

To ensure victory over the powerful Formorians, certain magical instruments were still needed.  Lugh sent out several men on a quest to obtain them at great price, and Lugh’s father died in their pursuit.  But obtain them they did, and just in time.  The Second Battle of Mag Tuireadh began, a battle of the ages, (battle sound effects) and “fearful indeed was the thunder which rolled over the battlefield; the shouts of the warriors, the breaking of the shields, the flashing and clashing of the swords, of the straight, ivory-hilted swords, the music and harmony of the belly-darts and the sighing and wringing of the spears and lances.”  Lugh’s agility and quick thinking won the day and true to the old Druid’s prophecy, Balor lay dead at the hand of his grandson.  Lugh, the redeemer of the Túatha Dé, was then enthroned in place of Nuada, at Nuada’s request.  The victory over chaos and brute force was complete, and all would be well in the Land for a time, as long as a worthy king sat in the High Seat of Tara.  (Much whooping and hooraying!)

 

Taken and adapted from Celtic Myths and Legends by T.W. Rolleston (pp.109-118)
